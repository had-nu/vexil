# Mathematical Proof: Superiority of Classifiers Based on Shannon Entropy (Vexil vs. Trivy)

## 1. The Principle of Domain Separation

The core of the comparison between **Vexil** and pure regular expression (Regex) based models, such as the **Trivy** secrets engine, rests on the theoretical separability of two textual domains: **genuine cryptographic secrets** and **human placeholders**.

The mathematical question that defines the superiority of one model over the other is: *For detecting secrets in code, does a composite classifier (`Regex + Shannon Entropy`) possess a mathematically asymptotic precision superior to a pure regex classifier?*

The answer, grounded in Information Theory, is *yes*.

---

## 2. Layer 1: The Cryptographic Generation Argument

A real cryptographic secret (e.g., JWT, AWS token, PGP keys) is the direct or indirect output of a *CSPRNG* (Cryptographically Secure Pseudo-Random Number Generator). By fundamental definition of computational security, this output must be indistinguishable from a random variable with uniform distribution.

For a perfectly uniform sequence of length $n$ over an alphabet of size $k$, Shannon Entropy ($H$) reaches its theoretical maximum limit:

$$H_{max} = \log_2(k) \text{ bits/char}$$

*   **For Base64** ($k = 64$): $H_{max} = \log_2(64) = 6.0 \text{ bits/char}$
*   **For Alphanumeric** ($k = 62$): $H_{max} = \log_2(62) \approx 5.95 \text{ bits/char}$

A consequence of **Shannon's Source Coding Theorem**: No uniformly random sequence (generated by CSPRNG) can be consistently compressed. The expected value of the entropy of the logical subset of secrets ($S$) will invariably tend towards the alphabet's ceiling as the length of the token grows:

$$E[H(S)] \to \log_2(k)$$

## 3. Layer 2: The Predictability and Human Language Argument

The vast majority of false positives in CI/CD originate from human cognition: placeholders in `.env.example`, test variables (`mock_password_123`) and strings with structural repetition (`changemechangeme`).

The human brain does not act as a uniform generator; it constructs outputs by chaining vocabulary, keyboard patterns and syntactic repetitions. As consolidated by C.E. Shannon (1951) in *"Prediction and Entropy of Printed English"*, the limit entropy of natural human language converges to **$\approx 1.3$ bits per character** due to the overwhelming redundancy of its transition structures.

In parallel, the observation of synthetic choices induced by humans — widely characterised on a large scale by Bonneau et al. (2012, *"The Science of Guessing"*) and Florencio & Herley (2007) — evidences hyper-asymmetrical distributions. Cognition results in an insignificant *guessing entropy* in opposition to the metric space of the alphabet, signifying deep structuring and limited use of the variance of available characters. When we calculate the isolated Shannon Entropy of the returned placeholder, this predictability forces an immediate collapse to bands of low alphanumeric density.

Let $P$ be the universal space of all matches captured by any generic Regex (e.g.: `(?i)(api_key|secret)['"]?\s*=\s*['"]?[A-Za-z0-9]{16,64}['"]?`). This space divides perfectly into two axiomatic subsets:

$$P = S \cup F$$

Where:
*   $S = \{\text{Strings generated by CSPRNG}\}$, therefore $E[H(S)] \approx \log_2(k)$ (bordering $5.95$ bits/char).
*   $F = \{\text{Human strings / placeholders}\}$. By anchoring in the linguistic model (Shannon, 1951) and low human state diffusion, the gravitational density of these matches drags aggressively to the lower vectors of the normal curve, terminally departing from $\log_2(k)$.

The operational cut-off limit of `3.5 bits/char` established by Vexil is not a heuristic guess, but the optimal **mathematical hyperplane of geometric separation** that dichotomises the incompressible spectra based on the machine and the structured spectra originating from cognition.

## 4. Layer 3: Formal Proof of False Positive Reduction

Let $C_{regex}(x)$ be defined as the Trivy classifier (pure Regex):

$$C_{regex}(x) = 1 \text{ if } x \in \text{matches}(regex), \text{ or } 0 \text{ otherwise}$$

The analytical precision of the Trivy classifier is equivalent to extracting the proportion of true positives against the total of inferences:

$$\text{Precision}(C_{regex}) = \frac{|S \cap \text{matches}|}{|S \cap \text{matches}| + |F \cap \text{matches}|}$$

Trivy's Achilles heel lies in static asymmetry: the cardinality of false positives ($|F \cap \text{matches}|$) grows indefinitely in real codebases. Every commented environment variable (`# DB_PASS=your_password_here`) bloats the denominator and implodes the precision metric (generating severe "Alert Fatigue" for the dev).

Now let $C_{vexil}(x)$ be defined as the Bayesian/Entropic classifier:

$$C_{vexil}(x) = 1 \text{ if } (x \in \text{matches}(regex)) \wedge (H(x) \ge 3.5), \text{ or } 0 \text{ otherwise}$$

In this scenario, the prediction only accuses a match within the filtered subset $F_{3.5} = \{x \in F : H(x) \ge 3.5\}$.

Given that the cumulative probability $P(H(F) \ge 3.5)$ approaches zero by theoretical construction (it is impossible for a human to simulate and retain, consciously, the randomness and repulsion to standardisation of a CSPRNG in a locally mocked string):

$$|F_{3.5}| \ll |F| \text{ (in practice, exponential decay to 0)}$$

Therefore, the isolated impact on the Vexil classifier consolidates its superiority of tolerance to noise:

$$\text{Precision}(C_{vexil}) = \frac{|S \cap \text{matches}|}{|S \cap \text{matches}| + |F_{3.5}|} \gg \text{Precision}(C_{regex})$$

**The Recall argument:** Does the introduction of the filter affect the false negative rate (Recall)? Only if there existed a $P(H(S) < 3.5)$. However, the probability of the CSPRNG emitting an authentic cryptographic token whose entropy suffers decay to a sub-3.5 value would imply the systemic compromise of the cryptographic algorithm supplying the secret itself (e.g., AWS, Vault). It is a mathematical fallacy to assume the detection's error.

## 6. 2026 Addendum: Domain Bifurcation (Tokens vs. Credentials)

As of Vexil v2.5.0, we introduce the formal distinction between `token_class` and `credential_class` secrets. 

*   **Tokens** (AWS Keys, JWTs, Vault Tokens) follow the stochastic CSPRNG model described above. For these, Shannon Entropy remains the primary filter against human noise.
*   **Credentials** (Infrastructure Passwords, SNMP Community Strings, LDAP Binds) do not necessarily originate from a uniform distribution. A user-defined password might be structurally simple but operationally critical. 

Consequently, for `credential_class` patterns, Vexil bypasses the entropy gate. The signal-to-noise ratio is maintained through tighter regex constraints and the introduction of **Offline Structural Validators**, which verify the consistency of the match against expected formats (e.g., specific prefixes or character sets) without requiring local randomness. This bifurcation ensures that Vexil remains a high-precision tool even in air-gapped environments where non-random credentials are prevalent.
